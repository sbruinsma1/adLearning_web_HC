Information about this study

What is the purpose of this study?

The main purpose of this study is to investigate the effect of motivation on behaviour across the adult lifespan, from young to older adulthood. Specifically, we aim to understand the brain mechanisms of motivation and the decrease in motivation, sometimes termed apathy, which is sometimes observed as we grow older. Recent neuroscience research has begun to shed light on the mechanisms underlying reduced motivation in people. This research has asked, what are the processes leading up to a normal motivated goal-directed action? This study will examine these different processes in order to characterise the specific changes that might happen when we get older. Eventually, our goal is to improve the clinical treatments that are available to improve motivation.

Do I have to take part?

No. It is up to you to decide whether or not to take part. We will describe the study to you in this information sheet. If you decide to participate, we will ask you to provide informed consent by checking a box next to a series of statements. You are free to withdraw from the study at any time without giving a reason.

What does the procedure involve? What will happen to me if I take part?

If you are interested in participating in the study, you will be invited to confirm that you are eligible to take part in the study. If you are eligible and would like to continue, you may automatically begin an online testing session on your computer. We will not save any personally identifiable information (PII). PII is any type of data that can be used to identify a particular person, for example, an exact date of birth or even your IP address. We will only check your IP address before you start the study in order to make sure you have not done this study already. Your IP address will be saved separately from any of the collected data in a general pool of IP addresses of people who have completed this study. It will therefore not be possible to link the IP to your data. All information saved during the experiment is encrypted using SSL.

What are the computer tasks like?

These are tasks where you view a computer screen and respond by pressing a button or key on a keyboard. They concern learning and making decisions in response to rewards. There is no “right” or “wrong” answers here, and we will ask you to simply do the best you can during the tasks.

What are the possible risks of taking part?

There are no risks associated with the questionnaires or computer tasks in this study. However, some people may feel uncomfortable answering questions about their mood or thoughts. The questionnaires on their own cannot diagnose mental health problems, such as depression, but if you score highly, we will recommend seeing your local GP or psychiatrist. Should you have thoughts of harming yourself, please contact your local care team, GP or one of the national helplines, such as Samaritans (116 123 in the UK). More information can be found at: https://www.nhs.uk/mental-health/feelings-symptoms-behaviours/behaviours/help-for-suicidal-thoughts. You can also contact the Principal Investigator of the study nwolpe@tau.ac.il.
